DynRouteNet: Lightweight Facial Shadow Removal
with Mask-Guided Dynamic Routing
Yinghao Zeng, Xuhang Chen , Zimeng Li, Xiujun Zhang, Mengqian Wang, Yihang Dong, Xiaofeng Zhang,
Chi-Man Pun, Kim-Fung Tsang, IEEE Fellow
Abstract—Facial shadow removal is essential for consumer
imaging devices, including smartphones, webcams, and smart
home cameras, where varying illumination conditions signifi-
cantly degrade image quality and the user experience. While deep
learning approaches have shown promise, their computational
demands limit deployment on resource-constrained consumer
electronics. This paper presents DynRouteNet, a lightweight
neural architecture specifically designed for real-time facial
shadow removal in consumer devices. Our approach introduces
two key innovations: (1) a dynamic routing mechanism that
adaptively allocates computational resources based on shadow
complexity, enabling efficient processing on mobile processors;
and (2) the ScaleFuse MaskFlow operation that leverages multi-
resolution shadow masks to preserve facial details while mini-
mizing the memory footprint. Extensive experiments demonstrate
that DynRouteNet achieves state-of-the-art shadow removal qual-
ity with only 1.113M parameters, enabling real-time performance
(<50 ms) on consumer-grade mobile processors. The proposed
method maintains robust performance across diverse lighting
conditions typical in consumer scenarios, from indoor selfies
to outdoor video calls, while preserving facial identity with a
structural similarity index of 0.947. The code is available at
https://github.com/nicholasdezeng/DynRouteNet.
Index Terms—Facial shadow removal, routing mechanism.

HADOW artifacts in digital imagery present significant
challenges for consumer electronics applications, from
smartphone cameras to smart home security systems. These
visual phenomena, while contributing to natural scene per-
ception, substantially degrade the performance of consumer-
oriented computer vision applications, including facial au-
thentication systems [1], smart surveillance [2], and real-time
image enhancement [3], [4]. As consumer devices increas-
ingly rely on robust image processing capabilities, developing
efficient shadow removal techniques has become critical for
enhancing user experience and device functionality.
Traditional shadow removal approaches have employed
predetermined strategies, including color model-based meth-
ods [5], illumination ratio techniques [6], and texture con-
sistency algorithms [7]. While suitable for specific consumer
scenarios with controlled conditions, these methods often fail
in everyday consumer environments characterized by complex
indoor/outdoor lighting, dynamic shadow patterns, and varying
capture conditions typical of mobile and IoT devices.
The integration of deep learning [8]–[10] into consumer
electronics has revolutionized shadow removal capabilities
through end-to-end frameworks [11]–[14] that can learn from
diverse consumer imaging datasets. These models lever-
age non-linear feature extraction and specialized loss func-
tions [15] to achieve superior performance. However, de-
ployment in resource-constrained consumer devices faces sig-
nificant challenges. Current deep learning approaches often
lack the robustness required for diverse consumer imaging
scenarios, from selfie cameras to home monitoring systems.
Static feature extraction architectures struggle with the wide
variety of shadow conditions encountered in daily use. Further-
more, while attention mechanisms excel at capturing global
dependencies, their computational demands often exceed the
capabilities of consumer-grade processors. The superficial
integration of shadow mask information in existing methods
overlooks spatial cues crucial for maintaining image quality
in consumer applications, potentially introducing artifacts that
degrade user experience.
To address these consumer electronics-specific challenges,
we present DynRouteNet, a lightweight end-to-end frame-
work optimized for efficient shadow removal in consumer
devices. DynRouteNet adaptively processes diverse imaging
conditions common in consumer applications, with particular
emphasis on facial shadow removal—a critical feature for
smartphone cameras, video conferencing systems, and biomet-
ric authentication devices. Facial shadow removal in consumer
electronics demands precise restoration of fine details (e.g.,

II. RELATED WORK
A. Shadow Removal
Traditional approaches to shadow removal predominantly
relied on hand-crafted visual features, principles of color
consistency, or the explicit modeling of illumination char-
acteristics. For instance, Tappen et al. [18] leveraged color
invariance for the joint task of shadow detection and removal,
whereas Guo et al. [19] proposed a method based on matching
shadowed and non-shadowed regions identified via superpixel
segmentation.
The advent of deep learning has catalyzed substantial ad-
vancements in shadow removal efficacy. ST-CGAN [20], for
example, employed a conditional Generative Adversarial Net-
work (GAN) for end-to-end shadow detection and subsequent
removal. DHAN [21] integrated dual attention mechanisms to
refine features pertinent to shadowed areas, thereby enhancing
their distinctiveness. Addressing the common challenge of lim-
ited paired training data, Mask-ShadowGAN [22] introduced
an unsupervised framework leveraging adversarial training.
Subsequent innovations, such as G2R-ShadowNet [23] focus-
ing on region-wise transformations and SGShadowNet [24]
incorporating style guidance, aimed to improve structural
preservation and generalization capabilities. More recently,
DMTN [25] explored feature decoupling for more targeted
restoration, while TBRNet [26] utilized a three-branch archi-
tecture to achieve more accurate shadow boundary delineation.
RASM [27] further refined boundary details and textural
consistency through the use of regional attention mechanisms
and specifically tailored loss functions. DenseSR [28] reframes
shadow removal as a dense prediction task, introducing a dense
fusion block that combines adaptive smoothing and texture-
boundary recuperation to simultaneously ensure illumination
consistency and fine-detail recovery. Our proposed method
distinguishes itself by achieving superior shadow removal
performance compared to recent sophisticated approaches like
RASM and DenseSR, notably with significantly reduced com-
putational complexity.
B. Facial Shadow Removal
Facial shadow removal inherits many challenges from
general-purpose shadow removal, such as the precise modeling
of shadow transitions, the restoration of consistent illumina-
tion across the face, and the faithful recovery of underlying
textures. However, this task introduces additional complexities
stemming from the perceptually sensitive nature of facial
regions, where even minor artifacts or inconsistencies can
significantly distort identity cues and compromise visual plau-
sibility.
Pioneering research in this specialized sub-domain includes
explorations of dual neural network architectures [29], meth-
ods that leverage estimations of internal facial illumination
characteristics for removal [30], and various unsupervised
techniques employing GANs [31]. CIRNet [32], for instance,
utilized cross-modality attention mechanisms to foster syn-
ergistic integration of color and illumination information.
Concurrently, FSRNet [33] explored the utility of frequency
domain fusion as a means to better differentiate subtle shadow
edges from intrinsic facial structural details. Nevertheless,
a prevalent limitation among many existing facial shadow
removal techniques is their reliance on static processing
pipelines. These static approaches often exhibit constrained
adaptability when faced with the wide variability in facial
morphology, diverse illumination conditions, and complex
shadow configurations encountered in real-world imagery. Our
proposed methodology is engineered to address the nuanced
requirements of both general and facial shadow removal.
It achieves this through a novel dynamic feature extraction
paradigm, responsive to input-specific content and shadow
mask statistics, the architectural details and operational prin-
ciples of which are elaborated in the subsequent section.
C. Dynamic Neural Networks
Dynamic neural networks, as discussed in [34], adapt com-
putation based on input characteristics to improve efficiency
in tasks like image classification. Recent dynamic methods,
such as [35], use adaptive routing to allow conflicting tasks
to select different network paths, both in spatial and channel
dimensions. Unlike these general-purpose approaches, we dy-
namically route features using implicit statistics. DynRouteNet
applies a mask-guided mechanism for facial shadow removal,
where our DynamicBlock routes based on shadow masks to
accurately detect shadow boundaries. This is a departure from
static or single-scale methods (e.g., [27]), as our ScaleFuse
MaskFlow improves adaptability, making DynRouteNet more
effective in handling complex shadow patterns.

A. Feature Extraction and Processing
The feature extraction and processing module of Dyn-
RouteNet forms the backbone of our facial image deshadowing
framework, designed to address the limitations of traditional
architectures like U-Net, which often struggle with complex
shadow patterns due to their reliance on generic feature
extraction without explicit shadow guidance. Our approach
introduces three key innovations: (1) a mask reuse strategy
to provide consistent shadow guidance across scales, (2)
optimized multi-scale feature fusion (ScaleFuse MaskFlow) to
integrate fine-grained and semantic features effectively, and (3)
a residual output connection to preserve identity-critical con-
tent. These components collectively enable DynRouteNet to
achieve high-quality shadow removal with low computational
cost, making it suitable for resource-constrained environments
such as mobile devices.
Given an RGB image I ∈R3×H×W and a single-channel
shadow mask M ∈R1×H×W , we concatenate them to form
a 4-channel input tensor X. This tensor is projected into a
compact feature embedding F0 using a 3 × 3 convolution
followed by LeakyReLU activation:
F0 = LeakyReLU(Conv3×3(X)).
(1)
The choice of a compact 16-channel embedding reduces
computational overhead compared to deeper embeddings in
traditional U-Net models, enabling faster processing while re-
taining sufficient representational capacity for shadow patterns.
The encoder processes F0 through three stages, each com-
prising DynamicBlock modules and a Downsample layer.
Unlike standard convolutional encoders that treat all regions
uniformly, our DynamicBlock modules leverage the shadow
mask M to guide feature transformations adaptively, focusing
computational resources on shadowed regions. This mask
reuse strategy, termed ScaleFuse MaskFlow, downsamples the
mask using max pooling to produce Mk ∈RB×1× H

This approach contrasts with methods like [36], which use
static masks or implicit attention without multi-scale syn-
chronization. By aligning masks with feature resolutions,
ScaleFuse MaskFlow ensures precise shadow boundary detec-
tion across scales, addressing the challenge of varying shadow
sizes and intensities that prior methods often fail to handle
effectively.
Each Downsample layer applies a 4 × 4 convolution with
stride 2, halving spatial resolution while doubling chan-
nels to produce feature maps Fk+1. The synchronized mask
Mk guides each DynamicBlock, enhancing focus on shadow
boundaries and improving robustness to complex shadow
patterns.
The decoder mirrors the encoder, using Upsample layers
and skip connections to implement ScaleFuse MaskFlow’s
multi-scale fusion. Each Upsample layer applies a 2 × 2
transposed convolution with stride 2, doubling resolution while
halving channels. Skip connections concatenate upsampled
features with encoder features at the same scale, enabling
the integration of shallow, fine-grained details (e.g., textures)
with deep, semantic features (e.g., facial structures). This
fusion, guided by Mk, outperforms traditional U-Net skip
connections, which lack explicit shadow guidance and often
blur shadow boundaries during upsampling.
The final feature map is processed by the OutputProj
module, a 3 × 3 convolution producing the output image
ˆI ∈R3×H×W . A residual connection adds the input image
I to preserve non-shadowed content:
ˆI = OutputProj(Ffinal) + I.
(3)
This residual connection, inspired by [37], ensures identity
preservation in non-shadowed regions, a critical improve-
ment over methods that may over-process unshadowed ar-
eas, leading to artifacts. Compared to prior architectures,
DynRouteNet’s mask-guided processing and efficient fusion
achieve superior shadow removal.
B. Dynamic Routing Mechanism
DynRouteNet’s dynamic routing mechanism adapts feature
processing to the unique characteristics of shadowed facial im-
ages, preserving identity-critical features like textures and con-
tours. Unlike static attention mechanisms or generic adaptive
frameworks, which rely on implicit feature statistics and may
overgeneralize across diverse shadow patterns, DynRouteNet
explicitly uses synchronized shadow masks to guide feature
transformations. This DynamicBlock module dynamically se-
lects between a Channel Attention Block (CAB) [38] and
a Separable Convolution (SepConv2d) [39] based on input
features and mask characteristics, ensuring targeted refinement
of shadowed regions.

IV. EXPERIMENT
A. Dataset
The performance of our proposed method is evaluated
on two publicly available facial shadow removal datasets:
SHAREL [1] and UCB [29]. From the SHAREL dataset,
1026 shadow/shadow-free image pairs were selected, while the
UCB dataset contributed 100 such pairs for our experiments.
To evaluate model adaptability to varying image dimensions,
images from the SHAREL dataset were resized to 128 × 128
pixels prior to training, whereas images from the UCB dataset
were resized to 256×256 pixels. The datasets were partitioned
into training and testing sets using distinct ratios: SHAREL
was split with 90% for training and 10% for testing, while
UCB utilized a 70% training and 30% testing split. For
the UCB dataset, shadow masks were generated using the
preprocessing methodology described in [41].
B. Implementation Details
The proposed model was implemented using PyTorch and
trained on a single NVIDIA RTX 3060 GPU. Optimization
was performed using the AdamW optimizer [42] with an initial
learning rate of 4×10−4 and a batch size of 8. The momentum
parameters (β1, β2) were set to (0.9, 0.999), and weight decay
was configured to 2 × 10−2. To enhance training robustness
and prevent overfitting, standard data augmentation techniques
were applied, including random rotations, horizontal flips,
channel permutations, and MixUp [43].
To comprehensively evaluate model performance, we em-
ployed several standard metrics. Pixel-level accuracy and per-
ceptual quality were assessed using Root Mean Square Error
(RMSE), Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), and Structural
Similarity (SSIM). Feature-level perceptual similarity was
measured using the Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity
(LPIPS) [44]. Computational cost and model complexity were
quantified by the number of Multiply-Accumulate Operations
(MACs) and parameters, respectively. This suite of metrics
facilitates a balanced and comprehensive evaluation of both
the effectiveness and efficiency of the compared methods.

We conduct a comprehensive comparative analysis of
DynRouteNet against several state-of-the-art shadow removal
methods on both the SHAREL and UCB datasets. The

Quantitative evaluations are performed using Root Mean

Computational efficiency and model complexity are gauged

For instance, regarding LPIPS, our model achieves a com-

Despite its compact architecture, DynRouteNet consistently
delivers performance that is either on par with or superior
to existing methods, underscoring its suitability for real-
world applications where both high accuracy and operational
efficiency are paramount.
D. User Study
Deep learning-based shadow removal methods are typi-
cally evaluated using objective metrics such as PSNR, SSIM,
LPIPS, and RMSE, which primarily assess image quality
based on pixel-level differences. However, these metrics are
inherently limited in their ability to reflect human visual per-
ception. Although they provide useful quantitative indicators,
they often fail to capture critical perceptual factors such as
detail preservation, color fidelity, visual realism, and overall
image quality from the viewer’s perspective.
To address these limitations, we conducted a comprehensive
user study designed to complement the objective evaluations,
thereby offering a more holistic assessment of the visual
performance of various shadow removal methods. This user
study aims to collect direct, subjective feedback from human
observers regarding the perceived effectiveness, naturalness,
and visual quality of the de-shadowed results in practical, real-

E. Ablation Study
To meticulously evaluate the contribution of each key com-
ponent within our proposed DynRouteNet, we conducted a
series of ablation experiments on both the SHAREL and UCB
datasets. The results of this study are summarized in Table III.
The experiments demonstrate that removing either the
ScaleFuse MaskFlow module or disabling the adaptive nature
of the Dynamic Routing Mechanism leads to a significant
decline in performance across all six evaluated metrics. Specif-
ically, to isolate the contribution of the adaptive path selection
in the Dynamic Routing Mechanism, its routing weights were
fixed to uniform values during its ablation. The substantial per-
formance degradation observed in both of these configurations
underscores the critical role these modules play in achieving
effective shadow removal and high-fidelity image restoration.
Further component analysis within DynRouteNet examined
the individual effects of removing the Channel Attention
Block (CAB) and the Separable Convolution (SepConv2d)
module, which form the two dynamically selected paths within
each DynamicBlock. Ablating the CAB module resulted in
a marked drop in performance across the board. Conversely,
when the SepConv2d module was removed, a slight improve-
ment was observed in the LPIPS metric on the UCB dataset;
however, other metrics exhibited varying degrees of decline.
This finding suggests that while the SepConv2d module is a
key contributor to the model’s computational efficiency, its
presence also generally aids overall performance, indicating a
carefully considered trade-off in its integration.
Overall, this comprehensive ablation study validates the
individual effectiveness of each core module integrated into
DynRouteNet and confirms that the complete architecture,
with all components functioning synergistically, is essential
for attaining optimal performance.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we introduced DynRouteNet, a lightweight
and efficient network specifically designed for facial shadow
removal. DynRouteNet uniquely incorporates a dynamic rout-
ing mechanism that adaptively selects optimal processing
pathways based on input content, complemented by the novel
ScaleFuse MaskFlow strategy. This synergistic design enables
accurate restoration of shadow-affected facial regions while
maintaining a low computational footprint. Comprehensive
experimental evaluations on benchmark facial shadow removal
datasets have demonstrated that DynRouteNet surpasses exist-
ing state-of-the-art methods, achieving superior performance
across key metrics with significantly fewer parameters and
lower MACs. These results underscore the efficacy of our
approach in balancing high-fidelity shadow removal with com-
putational efficiency.
